Collecting Explicit Knowledge

Week 9’s resource includes knowledge mapping wherein participatory method was discussed. This video has been chosen because it shows an example of how participatory method has been applied to gather explicit knowledge from the community elders in Kenya. 

Crawhall (2010, in PPGIS) talks about how he facilitated an exercise using a three dimensional technique with map building and coding to a section of the Kenyan community. This was done to help the community understand their heritage and their history scoping environmental and cultural aspects.

The participatory aspect of the project was cited as the most significant part. Crawhall (2010, in PPGIS) talked about how intergenerational knowledge transmission where a lot of tacit knowledge that the elders hold can be lost overtime. This evidence-based resource is useful to note because it shows how vital explicit knowledge is thus during requirements gathering.

Lessons Learned in Requirements Elicitation

Knowing the common mistakes in requirements gathering is as important as knowing the tools to use. Whittenberger (2014) discusses the top human errors that hinders a successful collection of requirements and how it could be avoided.

I find the following lessons learned relatable to my previous BA experience:

  • Make sure that stakeholders are analysed and identified for times when representatives are needed when a new business area is identified. Also true during deployment, thus critical resources must be identified and secondary contacts recorded for risk management purposes.

  • Use business terms in creating requirements. I think it all depends on what type of requirements documentation is needed.

  • Facilitate a structured requirements walkthrough to get a faster common understanding amongst stakeholders. This should have been implemented on my previous IT projects to get a faster approval signages on documentations.

Agile Tools for Requirements Elicitation

In agile development, requirements documentations were less according to Solarte (2013). Thus, this resource has been chosen to show how visual techniques are used in agile practices. The methods cited in this video are used to present to clients and get what they really want instead of just asking questions and doing heavy documentations.

Since most of the organisations are now adopting agile methodologies (InfoQ poll, Paganini 2015), it is important to know the latest tools that can be used for requirements elicitation in Agile projects.
